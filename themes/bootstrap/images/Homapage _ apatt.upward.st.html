Sgt Hanno's Story

Staff Sgt. Erik Hanno lives on Fort Carson Army base in Colorado Springs with his family: his wife, Stacy, and their two little girls, Kailey and Tegan. As an infantryman in the Army, he served tours of duty in both Iraq and Afghanistan.

Sgt. Hanno's Story

Erik is a full-time soldier, while Stacy not only works full-time but takes night classes to get her nursing degree. Despite that, the family replies on federal WIC benefits to make ends meet.

“I want to provide healthy meals for my children. It's very expensive.”

– Stacy Hanno

Adrianna's Story

Adrianna, age 5, lives with her parents in Denver. Her dad works at a call center, six days a week. The family has a newborn on the way, and Adrianna's dad fears he will miss the birth of his second child.

Describing their situation, Adrianna's parents shed frustrated tears. Their budget is tight and they constantly have to choose between vitally important expenses. They have each often skipped meals in order to provide enough food for Adrianna.

Adrianna's Story

Adrianna started kindergarten this year, and it's an immense relief to the family budget, since she'll be eating breakfast and lunch at school. Knowing with certainty that school meals will provide two of Adrianna's meals each day will help the family stretch their limited food dollars when she's not in school, so she gets the nutrition she needs.

"If we didn't have federal nutrition programs my kids would starve. We would starve"

-Brandy, Adrianna's mom

Jahnique's Story

Jahnique, age 12, lives in Detroit with her mother and older sister. Her mother, who grew up poor in the city, works two jobs as a nursing assistant, and wants a better life for her daughters. Jahnique’s father no longer lives with the family, and money is tight.

Jahnique's Story

Though she typically works 50 hours a week at work, Marquel, Jahnique’s mother, still relies on food benefits like SNAP and free meals at school to help feed her girls. Without those lifelines, she says, her daughters might not be what they are today: athletes and straight-A students.

“I'm grateful that I have my children and that, even though they struggle, they still can excel. They’re still doing good in school. I'm very proud of my daughters.”

-Marquel, Jahnique's mom

About hunger in america

From city neighborhoods to rural farmsteads, families in every community across the nation struggle to put food on the table, or are limited to cheap, unhealthy foods.

The problem isn't lack of food

This country produces more than enough food to feed every citizen. In fact, as much as 40 percent of the food we produce goes to waste.

The problem isn't lack of money

We are one of the world's wealthiest nations, with ample resources to feed those without enough to eat.

The problem is a lack of knowledge about the hunger problem in America.

About The Campaign

Ending hunger in our lifetimes is possible. It will take public awareness and political outreach to build the necessary national will. It will take nonprofits, corporations and government, all working together with ordinary citizens.
Great Nations Eat is the start of an inclusive, unstoppable movement that awakens America to the terrible cost of hunger, and inspires us to commit to a solution.
Great Nations Eat is a growing partnership of nonprofits, filmmakers, media companies and others – led by MediaVest, Share Our Strength and the filmmakers behind "A Place at the Table".


A PLACE AT THE TABLE
Great Nations Eat was inspired by "A Place at the Table," a documentary film that tells the story of hunger in America by looking at its impact on the lives of real families. Directed by Lori Silverbush and Kristi Jacobson, and featuring well-known hunger advocates like Jeff Bridges and Tom Colicchio, the film was released in 2013 by Magnolia Pictures and Participant Media to critical acclaim.

"If another country was doing this to our children, we’d be at war.”
Jeff Bridges, Founder of the End Hunger Network and National Spokesman for No Kid Hungry
